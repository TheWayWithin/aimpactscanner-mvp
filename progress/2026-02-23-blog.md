# We Audited Our Own Marketing Claims — And Found Out We'd Been Lying to Ourselves

**Date:** February 23, 2026
**Project:** AImpactScanner
**Author:** Jamie Watters (@Jamie_within)

---

The last 36 hours have been one of those sessions where you go in planning to write a pricing doc and come out questioning every number on your website.

Here's what happened.

## The Task: Document the Pricing

Simple enough. AImpactScanner needed a proper `pricing.md` — tier breakdowns, monthly and annual costs, the value chain, FOMO triggers for each tier, how we move users up the ladder. Standard SaaS pricing documentation.

I had Claude pull every pricing reference from the codebase and build the doc. Four tiers (Free, Solo, Growth, Scale), annual incentives, upgrade triggers, the works. Looked great.

Then I started cross-referencing.

## The Discovery: Nothing Matched

I had marketing copy from the business side making specific claims. I asked a simple question: **are these accurate?**

The answer was: mostly no.

### The Price Problem

Every price in the marketing copy was wrong:

| Claim | Reality |
|---|---|
| Solo at $9.95/mo | Actually $5.95/mo in Stripe |
| Growth at $19.95/mo | Actually $17.95/mo in Stripe |
| Scale at $39.95/mo | Actually $34.95/mo in Stripe |

Three different prices in three different places — the marketing docs, the frontend components, and the Stripe configuration. None of them agreed.

### The Factor Count Problem

This one was worse. Across our codebase and documentation, we were claiming:

- **27 factors** (architecture.md, product-description.md)
- **22 factors** (PricingComparison.jsx, UpsellGrowth.jsx)
- **18 factors** (README.md, some product docs)
- **"9 pillars"** (architecture.md, product-description.md)
- **"8 pillars"** (README.md, other docs)

Five different numbers for the same thing, scattered across the codebase. Every document confidently stated its number as fact. All of them couldn't be right.

## Digging Into the Code

We went to the source of truth — the actual analysis engine. `supabase/functions/analyze-page/index.ts`. Line by line.

**What actually runs:**

- **18 MASTERY-AI factors** across **8 pillars** (M, A, S, T, E, R, Y, AI) — these produce the scored analysis
- **9 foundational SEO checks** — these are flagged as prerequisites but are NOT part of the MASTERY-AI framework score

Total: 27 things execute. But they're not all the same thing.

**The pillar problem was even more interesting.** When Sprint 2 added the 9 traditional SEO checks, someone gave them made-up pillar codes: `TS` (Traditional SEO) and `P` (Performance). These don't exist in the MASTERY-AI framework. The framework is 8 pillars. Full stop. You can't just invent new ones.

And because `TS` wasn't in the scoring object, those 8 factors were running but **their scores were being thrown away**. They didn't affect the overall score at all. A silent bug hiding behind a naming convention.

## The "Published Methodology" Claim

Our marketing's dramatic difference was: *"The only AI search diagnostic with a published methodology."*

We checked. The methodology documentation exists internally — detailed docs in `/docs/` covering the full MASTERY-AI framework. But **none of it is published publicly**. The core of our competitive positioning was a claim that would collapse the moment someone tried to verify it.

## What We Fixed

1. **Aligned factor counts everywhere** — "18 MASTERY-AI factors across 8 pillars + 9 foundational SEO checks" is the accurate description
2. **Updated pricing.md** — removed all "22-factor" and "27-factor" references, corrected pillar counts
3. **Updated product-description.md** — fixed executive summary, pillar list, framework evolution path, tier descriptions
4. **Identified the TS pillar scoring bug** — 8 factors analysing but not contributing to scores
5. **Flagged the published methodology gap** — needs to be actually published before we can claim it

## Then the Pricing Strategy Changed

Right after fixing the documentation, a new unified pricing strategy dropped from the business side. Everything moved:

- All prices going up (Solo $5.95 → $9.95, Growth $17.95 → $19.95, Scale $34.95 → $39.95)
- Annual discount changing from ~30% to 25% ("3 months free" framing)
- All existing Stripe price IDs deprecated
- LLM.txt Mastery bundled free at Growth+ tiers
- Every scarcity tactic being removed (countdown timers, fake visitor counters, fake price warnings, unverified social proof metrics)

The new strategy is philosophically different: value before gate, no manufactured urgency, all metrics must be verifiable. Which is a better product but means ripping out code that's currently live.

## The Lesson

**Your documentation lies to you.** Not maliciously — it just drifts. Every sprint adds features, every marketing push rounds up numbers, every stakeholder presentation inflates claims slightly. Over time, you end up with five different "truths" about the same product, all confidently stated, none matching the code.

The fix isn't better documentation. It's **regular audits where you trace every claim back to the source code.** If the code says 18 factors, that's 18 factors. Not 22. Not 27. 18.

The other lesson: **if your competitive advantage depends on a claim, verify the claim is actually true before building your positioning around it.** "Published methodology" is a killer differentiator — if the methodology is actually published. Ours wasn't.

## What's Next

1. **Create new Stripe price objects** for v2 pricing (6 new prices, all v1 IDs deprecated)
2. **Update all frontend pricing components** to new prices
3. **Remove scarcity tactics** — countdown timers, fake metrics, visitor counters
4. **Publish the MASTERY-AI framework** publicly (required before claiming "published methodology")
5. **Fix the TS pillar scoring bug** — either map those factors to real MASTERY-AI pillars or explicitly exclude them from the score calculation with documentation explaining why
6. **Implement cross-product bundling** — LLM.txt Mastery included at Growth+ tiers

The pricing change alone touches at minimum: `PricingTiers.jsx`, `PricingPage.jsx`, `PricingComparison.jsx`, `useBillingPricing.js`, `TierSelection.jsx`, `create-checkout-session/index.ts`, and probably a dozen other files referencing prices.

Not a small job. But at least now we know what the numbers actually are.
